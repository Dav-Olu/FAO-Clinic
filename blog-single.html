One Dentist for 54,000 Nigerians: NDA Sounds Alarm on Oral Health Crisis
  • September 23, 2025
  • Punch Healthwise
  • 5 min read

In a country of over 200 million people, the Nigerian Dental Association (NDA) has sounded a sobering alarm: there is only one qualified dentist for every 54,000 Nigerians. This staggering statistic, reported by Punch Healthwise, exposes a critical gap in Nigeria’s healthcare system and raises urgent questions about the nation’s commitment to oral health

Nigeria’s oral healthcare system is facing a critical juncture. With just one dentist available for every 54,000 citizens, the Nigerian Dental Association (NDA) has sounded the alarm on what it calls a “public health emergency.” This ratio starkly contrasts with the World Health Organization’s recommendation of one dentist per 5,000 people, underscoring the urgent need for systemic reform and investment in dental care infrastructure.

There are two things in life that a sage must preserve at every sacrifice—the coats of his stomach and the enamel of his teeth. Some evils admit of consolations, but there are no comforters for dyspepsia and the toothache.

Henry Bulwer - Quote

In response to mounting concerns, the Federal Government has launched the country’s first-ever Dental Technologists Summit in Abuja. The landmark event brought together professionals, policymakers, and educators to chart a new course for dental technology, workforce development, and innovation. The summit emphasized the importance of training, regulation, and integration of dental technologists into Nigeria’s broader healthcare strategy.

Amid the crisis, some pioneers are pushing boundaries. Dr. Sunday Atoyebi is leading a digital dentistry revolution, having successfully connected over 10,000 patients across West Africa through tele-dental platforms. His work demonstrates the transformative potential of technology in expanding access, especially in underserved regions.

Efforts to bridge the rural divide are also gaining momentum. Mobile dental clinics are being deployed to remote communities, offering preventive care, basic treatments, and oral health education. These clinics are a lifeline for millions who would otherwise go without dental services, and they represent a scalable model for reaching Nigeria’s most vulnerable populations.

Together, these developments reflect a growing awareness and commitment to tackling Nigeria’s oral health crisis. But experts warn that without sustained funding, policy support, and public engagement, the gap will continue to widen.
